Introduction

In order to demonstrate my pipeline's efficacy, I wanted to display key findings by highlighting an important case study.

Smoothing out the daily variability

One challenge with modelling (and subsequently forecasting) climactic conditions is the impact of seasonal variation. Linear modelling falls short for a number of reasons - predominantly due to the plethora of external variables (such as the AMOC = Antlantic Meridonal Overturning Circulation which is responsbile for much of the European weather patterns). In order to reduce the effect of some of these short term fluctuations, I created moving average records for each of the variables being studied across a 5 day window.

Adjusting for seasonal variation

As demonstrated above, there are clear limitations to an overall linear model since it fails to capture the majority of within year variation. In order to improve the standard linear model, I used a backdating method to construct individual linear models for each day of the year. For example, across my dataset, I examined every single 1st Jan to determine the specific trends for each variable. This allowed me to model how one specific day of the year had changed. Whilst computationally intensive, this enabled me to create forecast 'expected' conditions for each day of the year based on the historical trends. Although there may still be significant variation around these expected conditions, this type of modelling is certainly more informative than standard linear modelling techniques.

Expectedly, with increased greenhouse gas emissions globally as well as regionally, more heat is trappd on the earth's surface. Munich suffers rising temprature coefficients for almost every day of the year. This results in the overall warming depicted above.

Conceringly for rain, there seems to be a convex nature to the expected rainfall line where seasonal variability seems to reduce around 2006 before continuing to increase into 2025. The reason why this is so concerning is not just that more extreme rainfall patterns may increase the risk of flooding and drought. Additionally, the results shown above are caused by rainfall coefficients moving in opposite directions across multiple days of the year - highlighting a shift in seasonality. This may result in major challenges to German agriculture where harvests may occur earlier or later than expected, ultimately disrupting the food supply chain and potentially increasing consumer prices.

Similarly to temperature, there appears to be an overall increase in windspeeds. This may actually signify an opportunity for wind farms to increase in productivity around Munich, potentially aiding Germany's objective of reaching net zero by 2045. On the flipside, it also indicates the presence of more frequent gale-force/storm winds which may inflict damage to infrastruture.

Overall: Extreme Weather Events are on the rise

To utilise these new seasonality-adjusted forecasts for expected weather conditions, I calculated deviations between expected conditions and the weather on each day. This enabled me to generate a pipeline which could calculate the number of days when an extreme weather event was detected - be that wind, temperature or rainfall related. I then aggregated these extreme days on a year by year basis, to demonstrate the overall change in extreme weather days.

The results below are clear. Even adjusting for increases in temperature and windspeeds alongside the increased variability of rainfall, there is still an increase in extreme weather days. Whilst this highlights the increasing difficulty in making accurate long-term weather forecasts, it also demonstrates that there are two phenomena occurring simultaneously for societies to adjust to.

1. Seasonal patterns are shifting: with warmer winters and summers, windier days and increased drought and flood-risk. This in itself requires governments to invest more in climate resilience in the short-term to safeguard food supply-chains and livelihoods dependent on them.

2. Even within these shifting seasonal patterns, there are more extreme weather conditions too. Sudden heat-waves, wildfires or freezing winters are more likely, alongside storms and flash-flooding. This again requires investment, particularly in infrastructure and advanced warning systems to allow people and businesses to better prepare.

These results in Munich highlight both areas for opportunity and concern. With a proactive approach, governments can begin to safeguard infrastructure and supply chains for years into the future as weather conditions continue to fluctuate more unpredictably. On top of this, there are also opportunities to expand into new agricultural niches as shifting climates offer opportunities for formerly hard-to-grow crops, as well as to generate more energy using wind power for instance.

On the other hand, these extreme weather events are calculated for a strong urban economy in a country with an ambitious net-zero target. One must also consider, resource limitations facing smaller urban economies across Europe and the increased stresses these weather conditions may inflict.
