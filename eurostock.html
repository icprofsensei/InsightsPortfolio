How is climate change effecting major European urban economies?

Why does this matter?

Climate change will become the greatest challenge many of us will face in our lifetimes. It's effects are already being faced rcently - 2025 has seen Spring heat records across Europe smashed. I want to investigate how global financial markets respond to these climate events. To begin this investigation, I want to investigate patterns in changing climate across European cities.

Which cities can we focus on?

Oxford Economics have created an international ranking of the 1000 largest urban economies. Amidst a number of different metrics, Oxford economics assesses economics, human capital, quality of life, environment and governance. My goal is to firstly assess the biggest European urban economies (which are therefore more likely to influence global financial markets the most) and then to investigate how their local climate patterns have shifted.

Maximum Temperature: Madrid

As one of the top 20 largest urban economies in Europe, Madrid has also seen an increase in extreme weather events across recent years. One such topical metric for measuring extreme weather metrics is daily maximum temperature - a metric which has surpassed 40 degrees celsius in recent summers. However, when observing this metric over time, there does not appear to be an evident increase in the daily maximum temperature beyond seasonal variation.

By observing the annual average for daily maximum temperature, this trend becomes slightly more visible - particularly between the annual minimum and annual maximum. To determine the extent of any changes, linear models must be built.

Maximum Temperature across Europe

Using linear modelling across over 8000 weather stations in Europe, I generated coefficient estimates for each linear model for each city's maximum temperature fluctuations. This revealed the following distribution:

It seems initially that the majority of cities show neither increase nor decrease in maximum daily temperature with a few outlying exceptions (possibly caused by limited readings at certain locations).

Therefore, I examined the linear models at the centre of the frequency distribution where the majority of cities lie.

Now, upon closer examination, it is now clear that the majority of sites fall to the right hand side of the no correlation line: they are indeed warming.

The Goltz 'Boiling Frog' phenomonon posits that a frog might allow itself to boil if it's surrounding temperature were to increase slowly. The majority of European cities have been slowly and marginally heating for decades.

What this means

As this introductory chapter demonstrates, even on the granular, day-to-day level, there is evidence of warming occuring in Europe. Next, I need to investigate how this warming particularly effects European economic capitals, through extreme weather periods which put increasing strains on a city's infrastructure and resources.
