The European rise in solar power

Introduction

In the past I have continually painted an ominous outlook for the extreme weather patterns threatening European urban economies. In the news in general, it is easy to be swept away entirely with the threats from wildfires, draught and food security.

With the same token, it is also important to reflect cautious optimism when signs of progress can be seen. That is not to ignore the global climate emergency ,where countries across the globe are posed with an increasing need for climate adaptation, but that governments and systems are responding positively in some regards to these dangers.

As part of my ongoing projects studying European urban economies and their responses to our changing climate, I wanted to present these global trends through the lens of Europe. Despite enduring one of the deadliest, most expensive and damagings Summers in 2025, I would like to highlight some of the shoots of progress becoming visible now.

How hot was Summer 2025?

Summer 2025 saw an extreme heatwave sweep across Europe, with numerous heat-domes forming prolonged hot weather across much of the continent. June saw an intense heatwave hit record breaking levels in many locations across Spain, Portgual, France, Italy and Germany (The conversation, 16/7/25) causing an estimated 2300 deaths. As the graph above displays, for portions of June and August, temperatures reached an average of 30 degrees across all sites in the ECAD dataset. This highlights the intensity of the heatwave, particularly since many sites reached maximum daily temperatures above 40 degrees.

How did European energy generation respond?

As the graph above demonstrates, whilst most forms of energy generation remain relatively constant throughout the year to date, there are two interesting patterns which emerge. Gas fuel usage reduced in summer 2025 whilst - at the same time- solar energy generation also rose simultaneously - capitalising on the natural, seasonal increase in hours of available sunshine. This indicates that European countries are beginning to meet Summer energy demands through solar power energy generation, at the expense of the carbon dioxide emitting, gas fossil fuel.

What was the composite makeup of energy generation?

The same trend is highlighted through proportion of fuel usage across Europe. As the northern hemisphere summer brings increased sunshine hours, governments are capitalising on this abundant energy resource through increased solar power generation as a component of overall energy. The evident bulge in solar power generation demonstrates that solar power generation is moving from a simple small-scale form of energy generation used in small properties and devices, to a method of large-scale, grid-supporting energy generation across periods of high sunshine.

How have fuel compositions shifted?

This is most acutely demonstrated in the graph above. When observing the year-by-year trends in energy generation, it becomes evident that momentum is building behind solar power generation across Europe. As the net proportion of coal energy generation has decreased in recent years, there has been a dramatic rise in renewables such as wind and solar, which are now comprising an increasing component of overall European energy generation. With this public sector shift towards renewables - and in particular solar - it would be expected that this would also be observable in the private sector, through supplying the panels, materials and grid infrastructure to support these changes.

Success study 1: First Solar

Although fundamentally a US company, First Solar are a global photovoltaic cell (PV) manufacturer who operate across Europe too with offices in Frankfurt, Germany. They are the largest Solar PV cell manufacturer in the western hemisphere, specialising in thin film technology over silicon panels. Whilst they dominate North American markets, they are yet to fully capture a large proportion of the European market . Despite this, as the graph above demonstrates (depicting their adjusted opening share price), their share price has continued to rise - indicative of global momentum behind solar power. With the growth of solar power generation across Europe, it is likely that their thin-film technology will rise in long-term value.

Success study 2: Nextpower

Nextpower provide logistical support for solar deployment. As demand for solar energy has risen, their services in design, deployment and operations will also prove essential in Europe. Nextpower so far have enabled 11GW of energy capacity across Europe and they run over 200 projects across 15 countries in Europe. Their products include trackers, software and module control systems to provide the technical logistic support required for large-scale solar projects.

Conclusion

Overall, this article demonstrates that in a changing world, European governements are beginning to respond to some of the opportunities presented with our shifting seasons. By increasing large-scale energy generation in the Summer months through solar power, governments can become less reliant on fossil fuels. I have identified two stocks displaying impressive growth in Nextpower and First Solar, indicating the global momentum behind solar energy generation. As this technology becomes more and more established across Europe (as the data indicates it may), there will be growing financial opportunities through investment in solar stocks such as the ones above.
